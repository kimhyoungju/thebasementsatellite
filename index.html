The Basement Satellite

"How far should you go in pursuit of a dream?"

Synopsis

South Korean artist SONG Hojun dreams of building a satellite and launching it up into space. In his underground art studio in Mangwon-dong, Seoul, he establishes OSSI (Open Source Satellite Initiative) and devises a plan to become the world’s first private citizen to successfully launch a satellite. He signs a contract with a French company called NovaNano which provides an adapter to install his satellite on a Russian Soyuz rocket. He also launches an effort to sell 10,000 OSSI T-shirts to raise the 100 million won (US$96,000) budget, but things get complicated. Online sales take a lot of work, the launch date keeps being delayed, and the technical challenge of constructing a satellite also drives him into a tight corner. Travelling through France, Russia, Argentina and the U.S., SONG finally heads for Baikonur Cosmodrome in Kazakhstan. Can SONG's dream be realized?

Trailer

“Art today isn’t just painting a pretty picture or making a sculpture. Making you think about something new and making something different is also art.” A South Korean artist and amateur scientist sets out to build the first civilian satellite. Blurring the lines between science and fantasy, Hojun Song establishes the Open Source Satellite Initiative in an effort to demonstrate that the most out there, seemingly unachievable dreams can come true. He spends five years testing, tweaking, drawing up diagrams and soldering circuit boards in pursuit of a goal that’s as crazy as it is inspiring. Will Song’s masterpiece achieve lift-off when it arrives on the tarmac in Kazakhstan? Does it even matter? Strangely compelling, The Basement Satellite stands as the ultimate all-nighter, a fluorescent-lit reflection on failure’s necessary involvement in the creative process and the struggle to try something new.

Angie Driscoll, International and Senior Shorts Programmer, HOT DOCS

Filmmakers

DIRECTOR | KIM Hyoungju

KIM Hyoungju has built his career as a cinematographer. He worked on feature films Pruning the Grapevine (2006) She Came From (2010) A Reason to Live (2011) and the documentary film Talking Architect (2011) among others. The Basement Satellite is his first feature documentary film.

EXECUTIVE PRODUCER | HAN Sunhee

After working as a film journalist and chief editor for local publications, Sunhee contributed for Variety magazine as their Korea correspondent for more than four years. Subsequently, she has produced theatrical documentaries including Talking Architect (2011) and Manshin: Ten Thousand Spirits (2013). She also produced a TV documentary Shaman of the Sea (2013) for Discovery Channel Asia-Pacific Networks. In July 2013, she established her own production company Gong Pictures and has developed several documentary projects for theatrical release and TV channels.
